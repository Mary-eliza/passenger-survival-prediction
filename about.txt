# AeroSafe: AI-Driven Aviation Risk Auditor

## 🚀 Project Overview
AeroSafe is a multimodal risk assessment system designed to predict flight survival probabilities based on historical accident data. Unlike simple statistical models, AeroSafe utilizes a **Random Forest Classifier** trained on over **58,000 NTSB flight records** to analyze complex correlations between aircraft manufacturing, weather conditions, and flight phases.

## 🛠️ Tech Stack (The "God Level" Setup)
* **Core AI:** Scikit-Learn (Random Forest), XGBoost logic.
* **Backend:** Flask (Python) serving a REST API.
* **Data Processing:** Pandas & NumPy for cleaning 30+ years of NTSB data.
* **Visualization:** Leaflet.js (3D Geospatial Mapping) & Chart.js (Real-time Probability rendering).
* **Interface:** HTML5/CSS3 with a "Cyberpunk Analyst" Glassmorphism UI.

## 🧠 Key Features
1.  **Dynamic Risk Classification:** Instantly categorizes flights into *Critical*, *Moderate*, or *High Survivability* zones.
2.  **Geospatial Intelligence:** Interactive heatmap of historical crash sites using Latitude/Longitude data.
3.  **Strict-Risk Algorithms:** Custom scoring logic that penalizes "Death Trap" scenarios (e.g., VFR into IMC) to simulate realistic insurance auditing.

## 📊 Model Performance
* **Accuracy:** ~72% (Classification Accuracy on Test Set)
* **Dataset:** NTSB Aviation Accident Database (1990-2023)

8. Limitations

Historical Bias: The dataset covers accidents from 1990–2023. As aviation technology improves rapidly, the model may penalize newer aircraft models (like the Cirrus SR22) based on the accident rates of their older predecessors.

Reporting Bias: The NTSB database inherently consists only of accidents. It lacks data on the millions of successful flights that occur daily. Therefore, the model predicts "Survival Probability given an Accident has occurred," rather than the probability of an accident occurring itself.

1. The Heatmap: How does it work?
The heatmap on your dashboard is not random. It is a direct visualization of the NTSB dataset.

What it shows: It shows the exact locations of real historical plane crashes from 1990 to 2023.

The Data Source: It comes from dataset/clean_map_data.csv (which we created in the cleaning step). This file contains Latitude and Longitude for about 12,000 crashes.

How it gets to the screen (The Pipeline):

Backend (Python/Flask): When you open the page, app.py reads the CSV file and converts it into a JSON list (e.g., [{lat: 34.5, lon: -118.2, survival: 0.1}, ...]).

API: It sends this list to the browser via the /api/map_data endpoint.

Frontend (JavaScript/Leaflet): The JavaScript code loops through this list.

If Survival_Rate > 80%, it draws a Blue dot (Survivable Crash).

If Survival_Rate < 20%, it draws a Red dot (Fatal Crash).

In short: The Dataset provides the location, the Backend delivers it, and the Frontend paints the colors based on lethality.

2. The Logic: How does it decide "Safe" or "Danger"?
The AI (Random Forest) is looking for patterns in the 5 parameters you provide. It assigns a "Risk Class" based on millions of "Rules" it learned during training.

The 5 Parameters & How they Affect Risk:

Make (The Plane): The model knows that "BOEING" usually has multiple engines and professional pilots (High Safety), while "AMATEUR BUILT" planes often have higher fatality rates.

Phase of Flight (The Moment):

Cruise: Very safe. You have time to glide if an engine fails.

Takeoff/Landing: Dangerous. You are low and slow.

Maneuvering: Critically Dangerous. This usually means a small plane doing tricks or low turns.

Weather (The Visibility):

VMC (Visual): Clear skies. Safe.

IMC (Instrument): Fog/Cloud. Dangerous (High risk of disorientation).

Engines: 2 engines are safer than 1 (Redundancy).

Month: Winter months (Jan/Dec) carry higher risks due to icing.

The "Strict Scoring" Math: Even if the AI says "90% Safe," our app.py has a Strict Logic layer.

If the AI detects any significant probability of death (Class 0), we subtract points from the final score.

Why? In aviation, even a small risk is unacceptable. We designed the system to be pessimistic (paranoid) rather than optimistic.

3. The Big Question: What are we actually predicting?
This is crucial for your viva/presentation.

Are we predicting if a plane WILL crash? NO. We cannot predict the future. We don't know if a pilot will have a heart attack or a bird will hit the engine tomorrow.

Then what are we predicting? We are predicting "Survivability Factors."

The Analogy: Think of a Car Crash Test Rating. The rating doesn't tell you "You will crash next Tuesday." It tells you: "IF you crash in this car, at this speed, you have a 5-Star chance of surviving."

Your Project's Definition: "User, given that you are flying a Cessna in Foggy Weather during Landing:

History tells us that 85% of accidents in this specific scenario were fatal.

Therefore, we classify this flight plan as CRITICAL DANGER.

It means: 'This is a high-risk flight profile. If something goes wrong, it will likely be catastrophic.'"

Summary for your Evaluator:
Input: Flight Profile (Plane + Weather + Phase).

Process: AI compares this profile against 58,000 past accidents.

Output: A "Risk Audit" score. It measures Vulnerability, not prophecy.
